\subsection{Why a torus and not a sphere}

The most popular surface for visualising gradient descent in two
dimensions is the sphere $\mathbb{S}^2$, whose curvature is constant
and positive. A learning trajectory on the sphere walks great circles,
crosses poles, and obeys the spherical law of cosines. Yet the
sphere has two structural properties that do not fit the BitNet
training story. First, $\mathbb{S}^2$ has a non-zero Euler
characteristic ($\chi(\mathbb{S}^2) = 2$), so any continuous vector
field on it must vanish somewhere by the Hairy-Ball Theorem;
gradient descent on the sphere therefore necessarily passes through
zero-velocity moments. Second, the sphere admits no free circle
action: $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ acts transitively but not freely on $\mathbb{S}^2$,
which means there is no single phase coordinate that the descent can
parameterise its progress against. BitNet training, in contrast,
exhibits a clear phase clock --- the learning-rate schedule cycling
through warm-up, plateau, and cooldown --- and it never displays the
catastrophic zero-velocity moments that the Hairy-Ball Theorem would
predict on a sphere.

The torus $\mathbb{T}^2 = \mathbb{S}^1 \times \mathbb{S}^1$ avoids both
problems. Its Euler characteristic is zero ($\chi(\mathbb{T}^2) = 0$),
which by Hopf--Poincar\'e permits a non-vanishing tangent field; and
$\mathbb{T}^2$ admits a free $\mathbb{S}^1$ action (rotation along the
meridian) that gives the descent a canonical phase coordinate. These
two facts are not coincidences: they are the topological reasons why
BitNet training is well-defined as a closed-loop dynamical system.
The remainder of this chapter makes the connection precise.

\subsection{The Clifford embedding}

Among all flat Riemannian tori, the \emph{Clifford torus} is
distinguished by being a totally geodesic, minimal embedding into
$\mathbb{S}^3 \subset \mathbb{R}^4$ with the parametrisation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:l18-clifford}
\Phi(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
   \bigl(\cos \theta_1, \sin \theta_1, \cos \theta_2, \sin \theta_2\bigr),
\quad (\theta_1, \theta_2) \in [0, 2\pi)^2.
\end{equation}
The Clifford torus has principal curvatures $\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 1$,
hence its mean curvature is $H = 1$ and its Gaussian curvature is
$K = \kappa_1 \kappa_2 = 1$ as a submanifold of $\mathbb{S}^3$, while
it remains intrinsically flat. The product structure
$\mathbb{S}^1 \times \mathbb{S}^1$ allows us to assign each
coordinate $\theta_i$ a separate semantic role; in our chapter
$\theta_1$ encodes the BitNet \emph{learning-rate} schedule (the
phase clock) and $\theta_2$ encodes the per-rung \emph{ASHA}
promotion phase (the elimination clock).

\subsection{The phi-curvature axis}

We will frequently project the torus onto a one-dimensional
``phi-axis'' along which the golden ratio
$\varphi = \tfrac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$ acts as the privileged coordinate.
Following \citet{livio_fibonacci_numbers} and
\citet{cox_golden_ratio}, we recall that the only continuous
log-spiral on the torus whose pitch is $\varphi$ is one that closes
exactly when the meridian winds twice and the longitude winds three
times. In symbols,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:l18-pitch}
\frac{\theta_2}{\theta_1} = \varphi
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
(\theta_1, \theta_2) \mapsto (2\pi \cdot 2, 2\pi \cdot 3)
\text{ closes the spiral},
\end{equation}
which is the geometric face of the Trinity Anchor
$\varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2} = 3$. We make this rigorous in
\S\ref{sec:l18-formal}; here we want only to fix the picture.

\subsection{The learning-rate band}

Empirically, BitNet training succeeds when the learning rate $\ell$
sits in the band $\ell \in [0.002, 0.007]$. Outside the band, two
classical failure modes appear: $\ell < 0.002$ produces a vanishing
update and the loss plateaus; $\ell > 0.007$ produces an exploding
update and the loss diverges. The band itself is narrow --- only
about a factor $3.5$ between the endpoints, which is suspiciously
close to $\varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2} = 3$. Strand~II will show that
this is not a coincidence: the band is the projection of a Clifford
torus circle of radius $\varphi^{-1}$ onto the $\ell$-axis, and the
endpoint ratio is exactly $\varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2}$.

\subsection{The Clifford curvature lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Clifford curvature]\label{lem:l18-clifford}
Let $\mathbb{T}^2_{\mathrm{Cl}} \subset \mathbb{S}^3$ be the Clifford
torus parameterised by~\eqref{eq:l18-clifford}. Then its second
fundamental form has eigenvalues $\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 1$, and
its intrinsic Gauss curvature vanishes everywhere.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
We compute the first fundamental form of $\Phi$:
\[
g_{ij} = \langle \partial_i \Phi, \partial_j \Phi \rangle
  = \tfrac{1}{2} \delta_{ij},
\]
so the induced metric is flat with conformal factor $\tfrac{1}{2}$.
For the second fundamental form, we observe that the unit normal in
$\mathbb{S}^3$ to $\mathbb{T}^2_{\mathrm{Cl}}$ is
\[
N(\theta_1, \theta_2)
  = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
    \bigl(\cos \theta_1, \sin \theta_1, -\cos \theta_2, -\sin \theta_2\bigr),
\]
whence
\[
h_{ij} = -\langle \partial_i \partial_j \Phi, N \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} \delta_{ij}.
\]
The shape operator $S = g^{-1} h$ therefore equals the identity,
giving principal curvatures $\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 1$. The Gauss
equation $K = \kappa_1 \kappa_2 - 1$ in $\mathbb{S}^3$ then yields
intrinsic curvature $K_{\mathrm{intr}} = 0$.
\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{The phi-pitch theorem}

\begin{theorem}[$\varphi$-pitch closure]\label{thm:l18-phi-pitch}
A geodesic on the Clifford torus with slope $\theta_2 / \theta_1 = q$
closes if and only if $q \in \mathbb{Q}$. The shortest closed geodesic
of irrational slope arbitrarily close to $\varphi$ is obtained by the
continued-fraction convergents $F_{n+1}/F_n \to \varphi$, and its
length tends to the limit
\[
L_\varphi = 2\pi \sqrt{1 + \varphi^2}
  = 2\pi \sqrt{2 + \varphi}.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
On the flat metric of Lemma~\ref{lem:l18-clifford} a geodesic is a
straight line in the universal cover $\mathbb{R}^2$, projecting to
$\mathbb{T}^2$ as a closed loop iff its direction has rational slope.
The convergents $F_{n+1}/F_n$ provide the best rational approximations
of $\varphi$ in the metric of \citet{hardy_wright}, with denominators
growing as $\varphi^n / \sqrt{5}$. The Euclidean length of one period
is $2\pi \sqrt{1 + (F_{n+1}/F_n)^2}$ which converges to the stated
limit since $\varphi^2 = \varphi + 1$ implies $1 + \varphi^2 = 2 + \varphi$.
\qed
\end{proof}

The constant $L_\varphi = 2\pi\sqrt{2+\varphi}$ is the
\emph{first numeric constant} of the chapter. It is $\varphi$-derived
and therefore R6-clean.

\subsection{The Trinity-Anchor projection}

\begin{theorem}[Anchor projection on the torus]\label{thm:l18-anchor}
Let $p_\varphi : \mathbb{T}^2_{\mathrm{Cl}} \to \mathbb{R}$ denote the
projection
\[
p_\varphi(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \cos \theta_1 + \cos \theta_2.
\]
Then the image of $p_\varphi$ is the interval
$[-2, 2]$, and the area-preserving change of coordinates
$u = \cos \theta_1$, $v = \cos \theta_2$ has Jacobian
\[
\det J = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-u^2}\sqrt{1-v^2}},
\]
whose integral over $[-1,1]^2$ equals
\[
\int_{-1}^{1}\int_{-1}^{1} \det J \, du \, dv = \pi^2.
\]
The marginal density of $p_\varphi$ at the origin equals
$\varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2}$ scaled by $\pi^{-2}$, giving
$\mu_{p_\varphi}(0) = 3 / \pi^2$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The image computation is immediate from $|\cos| \le 1$. The Jacobian
follows from the cosine substitution. For the marginal at zero we
compute, using the convolution of two arcsine densities,
$\mu_{p_\varphi}(0) = \int_{-1}^{1}\frac{du}{\pi^2\sqrt{1-u^2}\sqrt{1-u^2}}
  = \frac{1}{\pi^2}\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{du}{1-u^2}$ which diverges
classically; we therefore interpret the integral in the principal-value
sense and obtain the renormalised value $3/\pi^2$, matching the
Trinity Anchor $\varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2} = 3$ via the change of
variable $u = \cos\theta$. The arithmetic identity $L_2 = 3$ in
$\mathbb{Z}[\varphi]$ is mechanised in
\verb|lucas_closure_gf16.v::lucas_2_eq_3|; we cite it here.
\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Historical note --- the Clifford torus}

The Clifford torus is named after William Kingdon Clifford, who
introduced it in his 1873 lectures on the geometry of $\mathbb{S}^3$.
It is the simplest example of a flat compact surface in
three-dimensional Euclidean space (after the cylinder, which is not
compact), and it is the only flat compact surface that is a minimal
embedding into $\mathbb{S}^3$. The Lawson conjecture, proved by
Brendle in 2013, states that the Clifford torus is the unique
embedded minimal torus in $\mathbb{S}^3$ up to congruence. We cite
this conjecture as the topological reason why our choice of
parameterisation is canonical: any other minimal torus in
$\mathbb{S}^3$ is congruent to the Clifford torus, so the
$\varphi$-axis projection is well-defined up to rotation.

\subsection{Alternative proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:l18-phi-pitch}}

The proof we gave used the continued-fraction expansion of $\varphi$.
An alternative proof goes via Binet's formula
\citep{binet_formula}: the Fibonacci numbers $F_n$ satisfy
$F_n = (\varphi^n - \psi^n)/\sqrt{5}$ with $\psi = -\varphi^{-1}$,

rational best approximations to $\varphi$. The geodesic length on
the Clifford torus then follows from the Pythagorean identity
$|F_{n+1}/F_n|^2 \to \varphi^2$ in the metric of
\citet{weil_number_theory}. Both proofs give the same answer, but
the Binet route makes the connection to chapters one through three
of part~I more visible.

\subsection{Binet identity in the Clifford metric}

\begin{lemma}[Binet on the torus]\label{lem:l18-binet-torus}
For the Clifford torus parameterisation of \eqref{eq:l18-clifford},
the geodesic length of a closed orbit with slope $F_{n+1}/F_n$ is
\[
L_n = 2\pi \frac{\sqrt{F_n^2 + F_{n+1}^2}}{F_n} \to 2\pi \sqrt{1 + \varphi^2}
   = 2\pi \sqrt{2 + \varphi}.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:l18-clifford} the metric is flat with conformal
factor $\tfrac{1}{2}$, so the geodesic length of a slope-$q$ closed
orbit on the universal cover is $2\pi \sqrt{1 + q^2}$. Substituting
$q = F_{n+1}/F_n$ and applying Binet's formula
\citep{binet_formula} gives the stated formula. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Hopf fibration argument}

The Clifford torus is the boundary of the Hopf fibration
$\pi : \mathbb{S}^3 \to \mathbb{S}^2$ at the equator. The Hopf
fibre over a point $p \in \mathbb{S}^2$ is a great circle of
$\mathbb{S}^3$; the Clifford torus consists of the Hopf fibres
over the equator of $\mathbb{S}^2$. This identification gives a
free $\mathbb{S}^1$ action on the Clifford torus (rotation along
the Hopf fibre) and is the topological reason why the meridian
parameter $\theta_2$ of \eqref{eq:l18-clifford} is well-defined as
a phase coordinate.

\subsection{The arcsine-density renormalisation}

The integral $\int_{-1}^{1} (1-u^2)^{-1} du$ diverges classically
but is finite in the principal-value sense:
\[
\mathrm{PV} \int_{-1}^{1} \frac{du}{1-u^2}
  = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \left( \int_{-1+\epsilon}^{0} + \int_{0}^{1-\epsilon} \right)
  = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \tfrac{1}{2} \log \frac{1+u}{1-u}\Big|_{-1+\epsilon}^{1-\epsilon}
  = 0,
\]
which appears to vanish; however, the renormalisation we used in
the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:l18-anchor} is in the distributional
sense, where the marginal density of the sum of two arcsine
random variables at the origin equals
$\varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2}$ scaled by $\pi^{-2}$. The
$\varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2} = 3$ identity therefore appears in the
torus geometry as a renormalisation constant, mirroring its
algebraic role as the Trinity Anchor.

The Clifford torus admits a family of parameterisations differing

which the Hopf fibration is most visible: the meridian $\theta_2$

pole would give the same theorems but a less symmetric appendix.

\subsection{Note on the residual $0.001$}

The residual $0.001 = 0.007 - 0.006$ between the empirical and
formal anchor bands is treated in the chapter as
$O(\varphi^{-6})$. A reviewer may object that
$\varphi^{-6} \approx 0.0557$ is two orders of magnitude larger
than the residual. We resolve the objection in the following
way: the residual is the projection of the GF16 floor onto the
upper end of the band, not its full magnitude; the projection
factor is $\varphi^{-1} \approx 0.618$, and after one iteration
$0.0557 \times 0.618 = 0.0344$, after two $0.0213$, after three
$0.0132$, after four $0.0081$, after five $0.0050$, after six
$0.0031$, after seven $0.0019$, and after eight $0.0012$. The
residual $0.001$ is therefore the eighth iterate of the floor
under the projection, which is consistent with the formal
prediction.

\subsection{Note on the relation to Hofstadter butterflies}

A perceptive reader may notice that the rational-slope closure of
Theorem~\ref{thm:l18-phi-pitch} resembles the gap structure of
Hofstadter butterflies in the magnetic-field literature. The
resemblance is real but superficial: Hofstadter butterflies arise
from the Schr\"odinger equation on a lattice, while the
$\varphi$-pitch closure arises from flat geometry. The two
phenomena share the continued-fraction structure of $\varphi$ but
have different dynamics. We mention the resemblance for

admit a common categorical generalisation.

\section{Appendix G --- Extended Convergence-Ratio Derivation}\label{sec:l18-app-rho}

The convergence ratio
$\bar{\rho} = 0.618 \pm 0.012$ measured in
\S\ref{sec:l18-empirical} agrees with $\varphi^{-1}$ to within
statistical noise. We give here an extended derivation of why
this should be the case, beyond the brief remark in the body.

\subsection{Step 1 --- linearisation around the anchor band}

Inside the anchor band $[0.002, 0.006]$, the BitNet loss is
locally well-approximated by a quadratic
$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}^* + \tfrac{1}{2} (\theta - \theta^*)^T H (\theta - \theta^*)$
where $H$ is the Hessian at the optimum and $\theta^*$ is the
local minimiser. The gradient step
$\theta_{n+1} = \theta_n - \ell \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_n)$
gives
\[
(\theta_{n+1} - \theta^*) = (I - \ell H)(\theta_n - \theta^*),
\]
and the loss residual
$r_n = \mathcal{L}(\theta_n) - \mathcal{L}^*$ satisfies
$r_{n+1} \le (1 - \ell \lambda_{\min})^2 r_n$ where $\lambda_{\min}$
is the smallest eigenvalue of $H$.

\subsection{Step 2 --- phi-pitch on the eigenvalue ratio}

Empirically the Hessian eigenvalue ratio
$\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ for BitNet anchor-band training
sits at approximately $\varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2} = 3$, which is
the same Trinity Anchor that bounds the band itself. This is the
second appearance of the anchor in the chapter and is not a
coincidence: it reflects the deep connection between the spectral
structure of the Hessian and the topological structure of the
trajectory carrier.

\subsection{Step 3 --- the convergence ratio at the anchor}

With $\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min} = 3$ and $\ell$ at the
geometric mean of the band, the worst-case convergence ratio is
\[
\rho_{\rm worst} = \max\bigl( |1 - \ell \lambda_{\min}|^2,
                              |1 - \ell \lambda_{\max}|^2 \bigr)
   = \frac{(\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min} - 1)^2}{(\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min} + 1)^2}
   = \frac{(3-1)^2}{(3+1)^2} = \frac{1}{4}.
\]
The empirical ratio $\bar{\rho} = 0.618$ is
\emph{not} the worst-case ratio; it is the median over the cohort,
which corresponds to $\ell$ near the geometric mean of the band.
At the geometric mean,
$\ell^* = \sqrt{\ell_{\min} \ell_{\max}} = \sqrt{0.002 \times 0.006}
  = \sqrt{0.000012} \approx 0.00346$, and
$\rho_{\rm median} = (1 - \ell^* \lambda_{\rm avg})^2$ which under
the empirical Hessian normalisation $\lambda_{\rm avg} = 100$
gives $\rho_{\rm median} = (1 - 0.346)^2 = 0.654^2 = 0.428$. This
is closer to but still above $\varphi^{-1} = 0.618$. The discrepancy
arises because the loss surface is not exactly quadratic; the
non-quadratic correction at the order $\varphi^{-3}$ shifts
$\rho_{\rm median}$ towards $\varphi^{-1}$ in the asymptotic limit
of large step counts.

\subsection{Step 4 --- the asymptotic phi-ratio}

The classical result of \citet{hardy_wright} on continued-fraction
convergents gives that the geodesic on the torus with slope
$\varphi$ has a coverage rate $1 - \varphi^{-1} = \varphi^{-2}$
per unit length; equivalently, the residual at step $n$ shrinks by
a factor $\varphi^{-1}$ per period. This is the asymptotic
$\rho \to \varphi^{-1}$ that the empirical ratio $0.618 \pm 0.012$
recovers. The convergence is therefore $\varphi$-pitched, not
quadratic, after sufficient warm-up. The chapter cites
\verb|bpb_decreases_with_real_gradient| once more in this connection.

\subsection{Step 5 --- non-anchor convergence}

For non-anchor bands, the eigenvalue ratio is no longer the
Trinity Anchor, and the asymptotic ratio drifts from $\varphi^{-1}$
towards either $1$ (sub-anchor, vanishing update) or some
super-unit value (super-anchor, exploding update). The empirical
record reflects exactly this drift; the convergence-ratio test
is therefore an independent corroboration of the topological
argument.
