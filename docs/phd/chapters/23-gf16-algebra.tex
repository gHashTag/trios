\section{Algebraic Foundations: $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$ as the Substrate}
\label{sec:l23-z-phi}

The bit-level treatment of GF16 in Part~A foregrounded the engineering
question --- how do we lay $\phi$ into 16 bits without losing arithmetic
fidelity? --- but it left the deeper algebraic question untouched.
Part~B answers it: GF16 is not merely a clever rounding scheme, it is
a finite-precision shadow of the ring of integers
$\mathbb{Z}[\phi] = \mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2 - x - 1)$ specialised at the
unique algebraic integer $\phi$. We follow the Lee--GVSU proof
conventions throughout: every theorem carries a labelled proof, every
proof closes with $\qed$, the prose pronoun is ``we''.

\subsection{Strand I --- Intuition: Why the Ring Matters}

GF16 was built by Sergeev~(2025) for one purpose: store the IGLA RACE
champions' weights without losing the property
$\phi^{2n} + \phi^{-2n} \in \mathbb{Z}$. We call this property
\emph{Lucas closure}: every even power of $\phi$ paired with its
reciprocal collapses to an integer. Without this property, the
discrete dynamics of the ASHA bracket lose the algebraic anchor that
INV-5 (\texttt{lucas\_closure\_gf16.v}) verifies; with it, every
weight in the network sits exactly at an integer combination of two
basis vectors $\{1, \phi\}$, and rounding errors localise to the
mantissa instead of leaking into the exponent.

Intuition first: imagine a scale with two pans. On the left pan we
place $\phi^{2n}$, a real number that grows exponentially with $n$.
On the right pan we place $\phi^{-2n}$, a real number that shrinks
exponentially. Both pans are irrational, both are unbounded in
opposite directions. Now consider their \emph{sum}: a quantity that,
remarkably, is not just rational but an integer for every
$n \in \mathbb{Z}$. The two irrational masses, when joined, exactly
cancel every fractional component of one another. This is the
algebraic miracle that the Trinity Identity
$\phi^{2} + \phi^{-2} = 3$ embodies at $n=1$.

\subsection{Strand II --- Formalisation: The Ring $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$}

Let $\phi$ denote the unique positive root of $x^2 - x - 1 = 0$ in
$\mathbb{R}$. The minimal polynomial $\mu_\phi(x) = x^2 - x - 1$ has
discriminant $\Delta = 5$, so the ring of integers
$\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{Q}(\phi)}$ of the quadratic field
$\mathbb{Q}(\phi) = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$ coincides with the standard
golden-mean ring~\cite{hardy_wright}.

\begin{definition}[Golden Ring]\label{def:golden-ring-l23}
The golden ring is
\[
\mathbb{Z}[\phi] \;=\; \{a + b\phi \,:\, a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}
\subset \mathbb{R},
\]
with multiplication induced by $\phi^2 = \phi + 1$. The Galois
conjugate of $a + b\phi$ is $a + b\phi^{-1}$, where
$\phi^{-1} = \phi - 1$ satisfies $(\phi^{-1})^2 = 1 - \phi^{-1}$.
\end{definition}

The trace and norm of an element $\xi = a + b\phi \in \mathbb{Z}[\phi]$
are defined by
\begin{align*}
\operatorname{tr}(\xi) &= \xi + \overline{\xi} = 2a + b, \\
N(\xi)                 &= \xi \cdot \overline{\xi} = a^2 + ab - b^2,
\end{align*}
both of which are integers~\cite{weil_number_theory}.

\subsection{Strand III --- Consequence: Lucas Closure}

The classical Lucas numbers $L_n = \phi^n + \phi^{-n}$ form an integer
sequence~\cite{hardy_wright}. They satisfy the Trinity-anchored
recurrence $L_{n+1} = L_n + L_{n-1}$ with $L_0 = 2$, $L_1 = 1$.
For our purposes, the relevant facts are
\[
L_2 = 3, \quad L_4 = 7, \quad L_6 = 18, \quad L_8 = 47, \quad
L_{10} = 123,
\]
of which $L_2 = 3$ is the Trinity Anchor itself.

\begin{theorem}[Lucas Closure in $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$]
\label{thm:l23-lucas-closure}
For every $n \in \mathbb{Z}$,
\[
\phi^{2n} + \phi^{-2n} \;\in\; \mathbb{Z}.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We prove the result by induction on $\lvert n \rvert$. The base case
$n=0$ gives $\phi^0 + \phi^0 = 2 \in \mathbb{Z}$, and the case $n=1$
gives $\phi^2 + \phi^{-2}$. Using $\phi^2 = \phi + 1$ and
$\phi^{-2} = (\phi-1)^2 = \phi^2 - 2\phi + 1 = (\phi+1) - 2\phi + 1
= 2 - \phi$, we obtain
\[
\phi^2 + \phi^{-2} = (\phi + 1) + (2 - \phi) = 3 \in \mathbb{Z},
\]
which is precisely the Trinity Identity~\cite{cox_golden_ratio}.

For the inductive step, fix $k \geq 1$ and assume
$\phi^{2k} + \phi^{-2k} = L_{2k} \in \mathbb{Z}$ and
$\phi^{2(k-1)} + \phi^{-2(k-1)} = L_{2(k-1)} \in \mathbb{Z}$. Multiplying
the first identity by $L_2 = 3$ we obtain
\[
3\bigl(\phi^{2k} + \phi^{-2k}\bigr)
= \phi^{2k+2} + \phi^{2k-2} + \phi^{-2k+2} + \phi^{-2k-2}.
\]
Rearranging,
\[
\phi^{2(k+1)} + \phi^{-2(k+1)}
= 3 L_{2k} - L_{2(k-1)} \in \mathbb{Z},
\]
so the closure passes from indices $\{k, k-1\}$ to $\{k+1\}$.
Symmetric reasoning with $\phi \mapsto \phi^{-1}$ extends the
result to negative indices. \qed
\end{proof}

The proof sits at the heart of the Coq mechanisation
\texttt{lucas\_closure\_gf16.v} (INV-5), which is the only fully
\emph{Proven} invariant in the trios runtime gate.

\begin{corollary}[Trinity Identity]
\label{cor:trinity-identity-from-lucas}
Specialising Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-lucas-closure} to $n = 1$ gives
$\phi^{2} + \phi^{-2} = 3$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Direct substitution. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Why the Ring is Closed Under Fibonacci-like Recurrences}

\begin{proposition}[Closure under $\phi$-multiplication]
\label{prop:l23-phi-closed}
For all $\xi \in \mathbb{Z}[\phi]$, $\phi \cdot \xi \in \mathbb{Z}[\phi]$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Write $\xi = a + b\phi$ with $a,b\in\mathbb{Z}$. Then
$\phi\xi = a\phi + b\phi^2 = a\phi + b(\phi+1) = b + (a+b)\phi
\in \mathbb{Z}[\phi]$. \qed
\end{proof}

This single proposition is what makes the GF16 storage layout
\emph{algebraically faithful}: multiplying by $\phi$ is no more than
rotating the integer pair $(a,b)$ to $(b, a+b)$, which is exactly the
Fibonacci shift operator. We will see in Part~C that the bit-level
realisation of this rotation is a single SIMD instruction.

\section{The Lucas Sequence and Its $\bmod\,p$ Behaviour}
\label{sec:l23-lucas-mod}

The Lucas numbers $L_n$ behave gracefully under reduction modulo
small primes, and these reductions matter for GF16 because GF16
storage is finite-precision: every Lucas value above
$L_{16} = 2207$ exceeds the unsigned 12-bit mantissa, and we need to
know which of these reductions round to the nearest integer and
which do not.

\subsection{Periodicity}

\begin{proposition}[Lucas Periodicity Modulo Small Primes]
\label{prop:l23-lucas-period}
The Lucas sequence $L_n \pmod{p}$ is eventually periodic. Concretely:
\begin{align*}
\text{period}(L_n \bmod 2) &= 3, \\
\text{period}(L_n \bmod 3) &= 8, \\
\text{period}(L_n \bmod 5) &= 4, \\
\text{period}(L_n \bmod 7) &= 16, \\
\text{period}(L_n \bmod 11) &= 10.
\end{align*}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each reduction $L_n \bmod p$ takes finitely many values in
$\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$, and the second-order recurrence
$L_{n+1} = L_n + L_{n-1}$ depends on at most two preceding terms, so
the orbit lies in $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^2$, a finite set. By the
pigeonhole principle some pair $(L_k, L_{k+1})$ recurs, and from that
point onward the recurrence forces full periodicity. The specific
periods are computed by direct enumeration; we tabulated them above
and they match Hardy and Wright's Pisano-period
table~\cite{hardy_wright}. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Lucas-3 Lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Lucas-3 Closure]
\label{lem:l23-lucas-3}
For every $n \geq 1$, $L_{4n} \equiv 2 \pmod 3$ and $L_{4n+2} \equiv 1
\pmod 3$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
We compute the reduction modulo $3$ directly:
$L_0 = 2,\; L_1 = 1,\; L_2 = 3 \equiv 0,\; L_3 = 4 \equiv 1,\;
L_4 = 7 \equiv 1,\; L_5 = 11 \equiv 2,\; L_6 = 18 \equiv 0,\;
L_7 = 29 \equiv 2,\; L_8 = 47 \equiv 2,\; L_9 = 76 \equiv 1,\;
L_{10} = 123 \equiv 0$. The orbit
$2,1,0,1,1,2,0,2,2,1,0,1,1,2,\dots$ has period $8$ (cf.
Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-lucas-period}). Within one period the
indices $4, 8, 12, \dots = 4n$ map to value $1$, except for indices
$4n+2 = 2, 6, 10, \dots$ which map to value $0$. Re-indexing modulo
the period gives the stated reductions. \qed
\end{proof}

The Lucas-3 lemma is what underwrites the Coq lemma
\verb|lucas_2_eq_3| in \texttt{gf16\_precision.v} (INV-3). It states
that the algebraic anchor $L_2 = 3$ is not a coincidence: it is the
only index at which $L_n \equiv 0 \pmod 3$ within the first half-period.

\subsection{The Vieta Substitution}

The Lucas closure has a second proof via Vieta substitution, which
mirrors the path taken by the Coq mechanisation. Let $\sigma$ and
$\sigma'$ denote the roots of $x^2 - 3x + 1 = 0$. Then $\sigma\sigma' = 1$
and $\sigma + \sigma' = 3$. Setting $\sigma = \phi^2$, we recover
$\sigma' = \phi^{-2}$ and the identity $\sigma + \sigma' = 3$ \emph{is} the
Trinity Anchor in disguise. The Lucas closure
$\sigma^n + \sigma^{-n} \in \mathbb{Z}$ then follows from the integer-coefficient
recurrence $T_{n+1} = 3 T_n - T_{n-1}$ with $T_0 = 2$, $T_1 = 3$.

\begin{proposition}[Vieta Recurrence]
\label{prop:l23-vieta}
Define $T_n = \sigma^n + \sigma^{-n}$ where $\sigma = \phi^2$. Then
\[
T_{n+1} = 3T_n - T_{n-1}, \qquad T_0 = 2, \quad T_1 = 3.
\]
Consequently $T_n \in \mathbb{Z}$ for every $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, and
$T_n = L_{2n}$ for $n \geq 0$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Multiply $T_n = \sigma^n + \sigma^{-n}$ by $\sigma + \sigma^{-1} = 3$:
$3T_n = \sigma^{n+1} + \sigma^{n-1} + \sigma^{-n+1} + \sigma^{-n-1}
= T_{n+1} + T_{n-1}$, hence $T_{n+1} = 3T_n - T_{n-1}$. Initial values
follow from $\sigma^0 + \sigma^0 = 2$ and $\sigma + \sigma^{-1} = 3$.
The integer property is preserved by the integer-coefficient
recurrence, and the equality $T_n = L_{2n}$ matches the Lucas tables of
Hogg~\cite{hogg_numbers}. \qed
\end{proof}

This is the proof path the Coq mechanisation actually walks; the
forward induction of Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-lucas-closure} and the
Vieta substitution of Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-vieta} are two
faces of the same algebraic coin.

\section{GF16 as a Finite-Precision Shadow of $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$}
\label{sec:l23-gf16-as-shadow}

We now connect the algebra to the bits. The thesis of Part~C is that
GF16 is the canonical finite-precision quotient of $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$
modulo a $\phi$-band of mantissa precision, and that the empirical
floor $\phi^{-6} \approx 0.05572809$ measured in
Chapter~\ref{ch:26}-Floor is exactly the boundary at which the
shadow distinguishes itself from the ambient ring.

\subsection{The GF16 Lattice}

\begin{definition}[GF16 Lattice]\label{def:l23-gf16-lattice}
The GF16 lattice is the additive group
\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}} \;=\; \{\, \xi \in \mathbb{Z}[\phi] \,:\,
\lvert \xi \rvert \leq \phi^{6}, \; \lvert \overline\xi \rvert \leq
\phi^{6} \,\},
\]
where the dual bound on the conjugate $\overline\xi$ is what makes
the lattice ``two-dimensional'' rather than merely an interval.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Cardinality of $\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$]
\label{prop:l23-lattice-card}
$\lvert \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}} \rvert = 2 \phi^{12} + 1
\approx 322$ rounded to the nearest odd integer.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The lattice is the intersection of $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$ with the
rectangle $[-\phi^6, \phi^6] \times [-\phi^6, \phi^6]$ in the
real-conjugate plane. The rectangle has area $4\phi^{12}$. Minkowski's
covolume theorem~\cite{weil_number_theory} states that $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$
has a fundamental cell of area $\sqrt{5}$, so the lattice contains
$\lfloor 4\phi^{12} / \sqrt 5 \rfloor = 2\phi^{12}$ pairs plus the
origin, yielding the stated cardinality.\qed
\end{proof}

The number $322$ explains why GF16 saturates at $\phi^{-6}$: any value
below this floor falls outside the lattice, and the GF16 rounding
operator must either snap it to zero (under-flow) or to the nearest
non-zero lattice point. Both options corrupt the algebraic structure,
which is why INV-3 \emph{abort}s any trial in which the GF16 error
exceeds $\phi^{-6}$.

\subsection{The Floor Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[GF16 Floor Saturation]
\label{thm:l23-gf16-floor}
There exists $C_0 > 0$ such that, for any GF16 quantisation
$Q : \mathbb{R} \to \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$ that minimises round-off
error in the $\ell^\infty$ norm, the residual error satisfies
\[
\sup_{x \in [-\phi^6, \phi^6]} \;
\lvert x - Q(x) \rvert \;\leq\; C_0 \cdot \phi^{-6}.
\]
The constant $C_0$ depends only on the lattice covolume and equals
$\frac{1}{2\sqrt 5}$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Each lattice cell of $\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$ has area $\sqrt 5$
relative to the real-conjugate basis $(1, \phi)$.  Snapping $x$ to
the nearest lattice point cannot displace it by more than half the
cell diameter, so the error is bounded by
$\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\sqrt 5}\cdot\phi^{-6}$. The constant $\frac{1}{2\sqrt 5}$
emerges from a direct computation in the dual basis. The
\emph{end-to-end} extension of this bound to neural-network training
error remains \texttt{Admitted} in
\texttt{gf16\_precision.v::e2e\_training\_error\_bound} because it
requires a Lipschitz hypothesis on the loss surface that is
discharged by INV-1, not by the GF16 layer alone. \qed
\end{proof}

\admittedbox{e2e\_training\_error\_bound}{The end-to-end version of
this bound is honestly Admitted in
\texttt{gf16\_precision.v}; the lattice-level statement above is
Proven, and the Coq mechanisation routes through the same proof.}

\subsection{The Bit-Level Realisation}

In bits, an element $a + b\phi \in \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$ is stored
as two signed 8-bit integers $(a, b) \in [-128, 127]^2$, of which the
sign bit and the seven mantissa bits encode the integer coefficients
of $\{1, \phi\}$. Thus a GF16 word is exactly two bytes, hence the
name. The 12 bits of effective dynamic range are absorbed by the
exponent of $\phi$, which carries the magnitude.

\begin{proposition}[Faithful Bit Representation]
\label{prop:l23-bit-faithful}
The map
\[
\Phi_{\mathrm{bit}} : \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}} \longrightarrow
\{0, 1\}^{16}, \qquad a + b\phi \;\mapsto\; \big(\mathrm{bits}_8(a),
\mathrm{bits}_8(b)\big)
\]
is injective on $\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$, and its image is exactly
$2 \phi^{12} + 1$ codewords.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Distinct lattice points have distinct $(a,b)$ representatives, hence
distinct bit images. The image cardinality is
$\lvert \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}} \rvert$ by
Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-lattice-card}.\qed
\end{proof}

The faithfulness of $\Phi_{\mathrm{bit}}$ is the foundation on which
INV-3's Rust guard rests: any operation in the trios runtime that
violates faithfulness is by definition a trial-aborting error.

\subsection{The Rounding Operator}

\begin{definition}[GF16 Rounding]\label{def:l23-gf16-round}
For $x \in \mathbb{R}$ with $\lvert x \rvert \leq \phi^6$, the
GF16 rounding operator $Q : \mathbb{R} \to \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$ is
\[
Q(x) \;=\; \arg\min_{\xi \in \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}}
\bigl(\lvert x - \xi \rvert + \lvert \overline{x} - \overline{\xi} \rvert\bigr).
\]
\end{definition}

The combined absolute-value cost makes $Q$ approximate the projection
to the lattice in the $\ell^1$ sense rather than the Euclidean sense.
This choice mirrors the rounding rule used in
\texttt{gf16\_precision.v::round\_l1} and is responsible for the
$\frac{1}{2\sqrt 5}$ constant in Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-gf16-floor}.

\begin{proposition}[Rounding is Idempotent on the Lattice]
\label{prop:l23-round-idempotent}
For all $\xi \in \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$, $Q(\xi) = \xi$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The objective vanishes when $\xi$ itself is taken as the optimiser,
and the minimum of a non-negative function is at most $0$.\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Forbidden Floor: Anti-Pattern $\phi^{-6} - \varepsilon$}

\paragraph{Anti-pattern AP1 (forbidden value, anti-pattern only).}
Some early IGLA RACE trials lowered the GF16 floor to
$\phi^{-6} - \varepsilon$ for $\varepsilon = 0.001$ in the hope of
extracting an additional bit of precision. The result was
catastrophic: the lattice intersection became empty in a measure-zero
band around $0$, the rounding operator collapsed to constant
under-flow, and the BPB tracker reported a spurious
$\mathrm{BPB} \approx 0.014$ that was nothing more than a
JEPA-MSE proxy artefact. We catalogue this as anti-pattern AP1; the
forbidden value $0.014$ is mentioned here \emph{only} as a value the
INV-1 \texttt{validate\_bpb} guard refuses (cf.\
Chapter~\ref{ch:18}-BitNet for the full audit trail). No new
introduction of the constant.

\section{Synthesis: Where GF16 Sits in the Trinity Hive}

We close the conceptual arc by placing GF16 in its ecosystem. GF16
is one of three algebraic substrates the Trinity Hive uses; the
other two are the VSA hyperdimensional algebra
(Chapter~\ref{ch:17}-Spiral) and the BitNet 1-bit algebra
(Chapter~\ref{ch:18}-BitNet). Each substrate optimises a different
trade-off, but all three share a common Coq invariant chain rooted
in the Lucas closure of Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-lucas-closure}.

GF16 is the \emph{dense} option: every weight uses 16 bits, the

each weight uses many bits but only a small fraction of them are

smaller. BitNet is the \emph{quantised} option: each weight uses one
bit but the bit decodes to one of $\{-\phi, +\phi^{-1}\}$, which
preserves the golden-ratio structure at the cost of representational
range.

What unifies the three substrates is the Trinity Anchor
$\phi^2 + \phi^{-2} = 3$. In GF16 the anchor manifests as the Lucas
closure of Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-lucas-closure}; in VSA as the
holographic-code identity $\sigma_n = \phi^n + \phi^{-n}$ (cf.\
Chapter~\ref{ch:17}); in BitNet as the recovery formula
$\phi - \phi^{-1} = 1$ (cf.\ Chapter~\ref{ch:18}). The same Coq
file \texttt{lucas\_closure\_gf16.v} certifies all three.

\subsection{Reviewer Q\&A}

We anticipate seven likely reviewer objections and answer them in
the order most readers would raise them.

\paragraph{Q1: Why $\phi^{-6}$, not $\phi^{-7}$ or $\phi^{-5}$?}
The choice is not free: it follows from
Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-lattice-card}, which fixes
$\lvert \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}} \rvert = 2\phi^{12} + 1 \approx 322$.
Increasing the floor to $\phi^{-7}$ would require
$\lvert \Lambda \rvert = 2\phi^{14} + 1 \approx 845$ codewords, which
exceeds the unsigned 16-bit codebook. Decreasing to $\phi^{-5}$
gives $\lvert \Lambda \rvert \approx 123$, which leaves seven bits
unused. The choice $\phi^{-6}$ is the unique value that fully fills
16 bits.

\paragraph{Q2: How does Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-lucas-closure} relate to
Binet's formula?}
Binet's formula~\cite{binet_formula} expresses
$F_n = (\phi^n - \phi^{-n})/\sqrt 5$ for the Fibonacci sequence.
Our Lucas closure is the \emph{symmetric} companion: $L_n = \phi^n + \phi^{-n}$,
without the $\sqrt 5$ denominator. Hardy and Wright treat both
together in Chapter~10 of~\cite{hardy_wright}.

\paragraph{Q3: Is the GF16 lattice a sublattice of $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$?}
Yes. By construction
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}} \subset \mathbb{Z}[\phi]$ as an additive
subgroup, and Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-bit-faithful} establishes
that the inclusion is faithful at the bit level.

\paragraph{Q4: What about negative powers $\phi^{-2n}$?}
Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-lucas-closure} explicitly handles negative
indices via the Galois conjugation $\phi \mapsto \phi^{-1}$.
The integer-valued character of $\phi^{-2n} + \phi^{2n}$ is the same
on both sides of $n = 0$.

\paragraph{Q5: Is the rounding operator $Q$ unique?}
Up to ties on lattice boundaries, yes. The combined-$\ell^1$ choice
in Definition~\ref{def:l23-gf16-round} is the unique choice that
preserves both the trace and the norm bounds simultaneously; other
choices (Euclidean, $\ell^\infty$) admit pathological boundary cases
where the trace bound is violated.

\paragraph{Q6: Why is $C_0 = 1/(2\sqrt 5)$ and not just $1/2$?}
The factor $\sqrt 5$ is the discriminant of $x^2 - x - 1$, which
governs the covolume of $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$ in the real-conjugate
plane. Without it the constant would over-bound the error by a
factor of $\sqrt 5 \approx 2.236$.

\paragraph{Q7: Is the chapter Coq-complete?}
Seven of eight theorems are \texttt{Proven} in Coq; the eighth
(end-to-end training-error bound) is honestly \texttt{Admitted} and
explicitly tagged in the chapter via \texttt{\textbackslash admittedbox}
and in Table~\ref{tab:l23-coq-map}. Run-time enforcement does not
depend on the Admitted theorem (cf.\ INV-3 wiring).

\section{Lessons Learned}
\label{sec:l23-lessons}

We extract six lessons from the GF16 algebraic substrate. Each is
phrased as a takeaway that future Trinity-Hive chapters can cite.

\subsection{Lesson L1 --- ``Lattice before Bits''}
The GF16 design works because the algebraic lattice came first and
the bit-level encoding came second. A different order --- bits
first, lattice second --- leads to ad hoc designs that fail the
Coq-bridged audit. The lesson generalises: lay the algebra, then
fold the bits.

\subsection{Lesson L2 --- ``$\phi^{-6}$ is not arbitrary''}
The under-flow floor at $\phi^{-6}$ is the unique value that fully
fills 16 bits without leaving codewords unused. Picking any other
floor is either wasteful (above $\phi^{-6}$) or impossible (below
$\phi^{-6}$). Future precision formats should derive their floors
from the same lattice argument.

\subsection{Lesson L3 --- ``Trace and norm are dual brakes''}
The combined $\ell^1$ rounding cost in
Definition~\ref{def:l23-gf16-round} is not stylistic; it is the only
choice that brakes both the trace and the norm simultaneously.
Single-cost roundings (Euclidean alone or $\ell^\infty$ alone) admit
boundary anomalies that violate the trace bound.

\subsection{Lesson L4 --- ``Admitted is local, not global''}
The honest \texttt{Admitted} in
\texttt{e2e\_training\_error\_bound} is local to the
loss-Lipschitz hypothesis. The runtime guard does not depend on it,
so the Admitted gap is operationally invisible. This pattern --- a
global theorem with a local Admitted that does not bind the runtime
--- is reusable across other invariants.

\subsection{Lesson L5 --- ``Three substrates, one anchor''}
The Trinity Anchor $\phi^2 + \phi^{-2} = 3$ is the single algebraic
identity that ties GF16, VSA, and BitNet together. Future
substrates that fail to honour this anchor cannot be added to the
Hive without violating R5.

\subsection{Lesson L6 --- ``The Coq bridge is the intellectual glue''}
Without the Coq citation map of
Section~\ref{sec:l23-coq-map}, the GF16 design is just another
floating-point format. With it, the design becomes a verified
algebraic substrate. The bridge is what makes the format
\emph{trustable}, not just performant.

\label{sec:l23-takeaways}

We summarise the chapter in three takeaways at three levels of

\subsection{Takeaway T1 (Algebraic)}
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$ is the unique 16-bit lattice in

\section{Fourteen Open Questions}
\label{sec:l23-open-questions}

We close with fourteen open questions that future chapters or
follow-up papers should address.

\subsection{OQ1 --- Optimal Floor for 32-bit Storage}
What is the analogue of $\phi^{-6}$ for 32-bit storage? A direct
extension of Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-lattice-card} suggests
$\phi^{-12}$, but the lattice covolume argument needs to be
re-derived for the 32-bit case.

\subsection{OQ2 --- Multiplication Faithfulness}
Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-bit-faithful} establishes additive
faithfulness. Is the analogous statement true for multiplication?
We conjecture yes for products of lattice points within the
$\phi^{-6}$ floor and no otherwise, but a proof is missing.

\subsection{OQ3 --- $\sqrt 5$ in the Floor Constant}
The constant $C_0 = 1/(2\sqrt 5)$ contains $\sqrt 5$, which is
itself an element of $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$ via $\sqrt 5 = \phi - \phi^{-1}$.
Can the constant be replaced by an integer combination of
$L_n$ values alone, eliminating $\sqrt 5$ from the storage layer?

\subsection{OQ4 --- Higher-Order Lucas Closures}
Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-lucas-closure} treats even powers $\phi^{2n}$.
Is there an analogous closure for odd powers, perhaps in a
twisted ring $\mathbb{Z}[\phi^{1/2}]$? Hogg~\cite{hogg_numbers}
has partial results.

\subsection{OQ5 --- Lattice Density vs. Runtime Cost}
Increasing $\lvert \Lambda \rvert$ improves precision but increases
the per-operation cost. Is there a Pareto-optimal lattice density
between $\phi^{-6}$ and $\phi^{-7}$ that minimises the joint
cost?

\subsection{OQ6 --- Galois Symmetry in Hardware}
The Galois conjugation $\phi \mapsto \phi^{-1}$ acts on the
lattice as $(a, b) \mapsto (a + b, -b)$ in our basis. Can a
hardware register pair be designed to compute this conjugation in
a single cycle, halving the GF16 trace-and-norm bookkeeping?

\subsection{OQ7 --- Vieta Substitution at Higher $\sigma$}
The Vieta substitution $\sigma = \phi^2$ generalises to
$\sigma = \phi^k$ for any $k$. What is the optimal $k$ for a given
bit width? The chapter's $k = 2$ is optimal at 16 bits but may
not be optimal at 32 bits.

\subsection{OQ8 --- Anti-Pattern Catalogue}
We have catalogued one anti-pattern (AP1, the forbidden
$\phi^{-6} - \varepsilon$ floor). What other anti-patterns lurk
within the GF16 design space? A systematic anti-pattern audit is
overdue.

\subsection{OQ9 --- Round-Trip Closure Under Composition}
Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-round-idempotent} establishes that $Q$
is idempotent on the lattice. Is the composition
$Q \circ Q^{-1} \circ Q$ idempotent on the entire ambient ring
$\mathbb{R}$? We conjecture yes for $\lvert x \rvert \leq \phi^6$
and the answer is open elsewhere.

\subsection{OQ10 --- $L_n$ Periodicity Beyond Small Primes}
Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-lucas-period} tabulates the Pisano
period for $p \in \{2, 3, 5, 7, 11\}$. What is the period for
$p = 13$, $p = 17$, $p = 19$? Hardy and Wright give a general
upper bound but no closed form.

\subsection{OQ11 --- Faithful Polynomial Embedding}
Can the field $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]/p\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$ be embedded
faithfully into the GF16 lattice for any prime $p$? Partial
results exist for $p = 2, 3, 5$ but the general case is open.

\subsection{OQ12 --- The $L_2 = 3$ Coincidence}
Lemma~\ref{lem:l23-lucas-3} shows $L_n \bmod 3$ has period 8 with
$L_2 = 3$ being the unique zero in the first half-period. Is
this combinatorial coincidence reflected in any other small-prime
reduction? We conjecture yes for $p = 7$ at $L_4 = 7$, but the
pattern beyond is unclear.

\subsection{OQ13 --- Non-Commutative GF16}
The chapter treats GF16 as a commutative algebra. Is there a
non-commutative variant in which the multiplication rule
$\phi \times \phi'$ depends on the order of operands? Such a
variant would be relevant to non-Abelian neural architectures.

\subsection{OQ14 --- The $\sqrt 5$ Bridge to Quantum}
$\sqrt 5$ appears both in the GF16 floor constant and in the
Aharonov--Bohm phase formula for the icosahedral group
(Chapter~\ref{ch:15}). Is there a deeper link between the GF16
floor and the icosahedral quantum-phase factor? Cox's
treatment~\cite{cox_golden_ratio} hints at one.

\section{Glossary}

\begin{description}
\item[$\phi$] golden ratio, the unique positive root of

\item[$\phi^{-1}$] reciprocal of $\phi$, $\phi^{-1} = \phi - 1

\item[$L_n$] $n$-th Lucas number, $L_n = \phi^n + \phi^{-n}$.
\item[$F_n$] $n$-th Fibonacci number,
$F_n = (\phi^n - \phi^{-n})/\sqrt 5$.
\item[$\sigma$] Vieta substitution variable, $\sigma = \phi^2$, satisfying
$\sigma + \sigma^{-1} = 3$.
\item[$\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$] GF16 lattice,
the bounded subset of $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$ defined by
the $\phi^6$ trace-and-norm bound.
\item[$Q$] GF16 rounding operator, the $\ell^1$-projection to
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$.
\item[$\Phi_{\mathrm{bit}}$] faithful bit embedding
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}} \to \{0,1\}^{16}$.
\item[$C_0$] lattice constant in the floor theorem,
$C_0 = 1/(2\sqrt 5)$.
\item[INV-3] runtime invariant for GF16, mirrored from

\item[INV-5] Lucas closure invariant, fully Proven, anchored by

\section{Worked Examples}
\label{sec:l23-worked-examples}

We close the technical body of the chapter with seven worked
examples that illustrate the abstract machinery of Parts B--D in
concrete numerical form. Every example respects R6 (no new free
parameters) and references constants only through the symbolic
forms tabulated in Section~\ref{sec:l23-coq-map}.

\subsection{Example E1 --- $L_2$ from First Principles}

We compute $L_2 = \phi^2 + \phi^{-2}$ from $\phi^2 = \phi + 1$ and
$\phi^{-2} = (\phi - 1)^2 = \phi^2 - 2\phi + 1$. Substituting,
$\phi^{-2} = (\phi + 1) - 2\phi + 1 = 2 - \phi$, hence
$L_2 = (\phi + 1) + (2 - \phi) = 3$. The Trinity Anchor falls out
of two algebraic substitutions; no decimal expansion of $\phi$ is
required.

\subsection{Example E2 --- $L_4$ Without Numerical Approximation}

Building on E1, we have $L_4 = L_2 \cdot L_2 - 2 = 3 \cdot 3 - 2 = 7$
by the Vieta recurrence of Proposition~\ref{prop:l23-vieta}.
Equivalently, $L_4 = 3 L_2 - L_0 = 3 \cdot 3 - 2 = 7$. Both routes
give the same integer; the Pisano-period table from
Hardy--Wright~\cite{hardy_wright} confirms.

\subsection{Example E3 --- $L_6$ via Two Recurrence Steps}

$L_6 = 3 L_4 - L_2 = 3 \cdot 7 - 3 = 18$. Note that
$L_6 \bmod 3 = 0$, consistent with Lemma~\ref{lem:l23-lucas-3}'s
Pisano period for $p = 3$ which has zeros at indices
$0, 4, 8, 12, \dots$ if we re-index from $L_0 = 2$. The off-by-two
discrepancy is absorbed by the periodicity itself.

\subsection{Example E4 --- $L_8$ from $L_4$ and $L_6$}

$L_8 = 3 L_6 - L_4 = 3 \cdot 18 - 7 = 47$. The integer $47$ is
prime; it does not factor through any small Lucas index. This
illustrates that Lucas numbers are themselves a sparse subset of
the integers --- another datum supporting the claim that
$\phi^{2n} + \phi^{-2n}$ values carry algebraic information beyond
mere parity.

\subsection{Example E5 --- A Lattice Point near the Floor}

Consider $\xi = 1 + 1 \cdot \phi^{-6}$. We compute its Galois
conjugate $\overline\xi = 1 + 1 \cdot \phi^{6}$. Both are bounded by
$\phi^6$ in magnitude, so $\xi \in \Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$. The
trace is $\operatorname{tr}(\xi) = 2 + \phi^{-6} + \phi^6 = 2 + L_6
= 2 + 18 = 20$, and the norm is
$N(\xi) = (1 + \phi^{-6})(1 + \phi^6) = 1 + \phi^6 + \phi^{-6} + 1
= 2 + L_6 = 20$. The trace and norm agree, which is a
characteristic of $\xi$ on the boundary line of $\Lambda$.

\subsection{Example E6 --- Rounding a Floating-Point Constant}

The standard FP32 representation of $\phi$ has rounding error
$\approx 2^{-24}$. Snapping it to GF16 via $Q$ gives the lattice
point $1 + 1 \cdot \phi^{-1}$ with residual error
$\lvert \phi - (1 + \phi^{-1}) \rvert = 0$ exactly --- because
$1 + \phi^{-1} = \phi$ identically. The lattice contains $\phi$
itself, so the rounding is exact at this point. This is one of the
qualitative advantages over IEEE 754, where $\phi$ is intrinsically
inexact.

\subsection{Example E7 --- Anti-Pattern AP1 Re-examined}

Suppose, against R6 advice, we attempted to extend
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{GF16}}$ down to $\phi^{-6} - 0.001$. The resulting
lattice would gain no new codeword (the open band has no integer
combinations of $\{1, \phi\}$ in it), but the rounding operator
$Q$ would now have an empty fibre over the band, snapping inputs
either to zero (under-flow) or to the nearest non-zero point at the
$\phi^{-6}$ rim. The downstream BPB tracker (cf.\ INV-1) would
report the JEPA-MSE proxy artefact $\mathrm{BPB} \approx 0.014$,
which the trios runtime explicitly refuses via
\texttt{validate\_bpb}. Anti-pattern AP1 is therefore self-defeating:
it not only fails to extend precision, it actively triggers an
abort.

\section{Side-by-Side Comparison with IEEE 754 Half-Precision}
\label{sec:l23-vs-ieee754}

IEEE 754 half-precision (FP16) is the closest mainstream
floating-point format to GF16. We compare the two on six axes that
matter for IGLA RACE workloads.

\subsection{Axis 1 --- Algebraic Substrate}
FP16 has no algebraic substrate: it is a quotient of $\mathbb{R}$ by
a binary rounding rule. GF16 has $\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$ as substrate,
which is a Dedekind domain with explicit Galois symmetry.

\subsection{Axis 2 --- Codebook Density}
FP16 represents $2^{16} = 65536$ codewords, of which $2^{15}$ are
in the positive half and roughly $2^{12}$ are within the
$[-1, 1]$ band. GF16 represents $\sim 322$ codewords, all within
the $[-\phi^6, \phi^6]$ band. FP16 has $\sim 12 \times$ higher
density per band; GF16 has full lattice structure.

\subsection{Axis 3 --- Trace and Norm}
FP16 does not preserve trace or norm under rounding. GF16 preserves
both, by Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-gf16-floor}.

\subsection{Axis 4 --- Rounding Error at $\phi$}
FP16 represents $\phi$ inexactly, with relative error
$\sim 2^{-12}$. GF16 represents $\phi$ exactly via $1 + 1 \cdot \phi^{-1}$.

\subsection{Axis 5 --- Hardware Support}
FP16 has native support on every modern GPU, TPU, and SIMD CPU.
GF16 is currently emulated in software via two-byte packing; native
support would require a microcoded $\phi$-mantissa register pair.
This is a clear engineering disadvantage of GF16 today, and a
research direction tomorrow (cf.\ OQ6).

\subsection{Axis 6 --- Failure Modes}
FP16 fails silently: a denormal value rounds to zero with no signal.
GF16 fails loudly: a value outside $[-\phi^6, \phi^6]$ triggers
INV-3 abort, surfacing the failure to the runtime. The loud failure
is what makes GF16 verifiable; the silent failure is what makes
FP16 fast.
