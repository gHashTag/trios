\section{Algebraic Foundations of Golden Imagery}
\label{sec:l30-algebraic-foundations}

Part~A surveyed the visual catalogue of golden imagery: rectangles,
spirals, vesicas, phyllotaxis. Part~B steps back from the visuals
and asks the algebraic question: \emph{what is the smallest set of
identities about $\phi$ from which every visual artefact in Part~A
can be derived?} Following the Lee--GVSU proof
conventions~\cite{cox_golden_ratio}, we present the foundation as a
short axiomatic kernel followed by labelled theorems.

\subsection{Strand I --- Intuition}

The golden imagery of Part~A reads, on first inspection, like a
zoological catalogue: a rectangle here, a spiral there, a vesica
elsewhere, with no obvious common thread beyond ``golden ratio
appears''. Strand~I undermines that reading. Every artefact is a
visual instantiation of \emph{one} of three algebraic identities:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the closure $\phi^2 = \phi + 1$,
\item the conjugacy $\phi \cdot \phi^{-1} = 1$,
\item the Trinity Identity $\phi^2 + \phi^{-2} = 3$.
\end{enumerate}
The catalogue is not zoological; it is a single algebraic family
visualised under different geometric projections.

\subsection{Strand II --- Formalisation}

\begin{definition}[Visual Kernel]\label{def:l30-visual-kernel}
The visual kernel of golden imagery is the triple
\[
\mathcal{K} \;=\; \big( \phi^2 = \phi+1, \;\; \phi\phi^{-1} = 1,
\;\; \phi^2 + \phi^{-2} = 3 \big),
\]
where $\phi$ is the unique positive root of $x^2 - x - 1 = 0$.
\end{definition}

The third identity is the Trinity Anchor; the first two are its
shadow on the multiplicative and additive structure of
$\mathbb{Z}[\phi]$. We will see that every visual artefact in
Part~A can be derived from $\mathcal{K}$ in at most three algebraic
steps, with no free parameters introduced (R6).

\subsection{Strand III --- Consequence}

\begin{theorem}[Visual Reducibility]
\label{thm:l30-visual-reducibility}
Every theorem in Part~A whose statement involves $\phi$ can be
derived from the visual kernel $\mathcal{K}$ of
Definition~\ref{def:l30-visual-kernel} together with at most one
additional structural fact from Euclidean
geometry~\cite{euclid_elements}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We establish the claim by induction on theorem complexity. The base
case is the Golden Rectangle ratio
$L/W = \phi = (1 + \sqrt 5)/2$: this follows directly from
$\phi^2 = \phi + 1$ via the closure of the golden ring under
quadratic recurrence, which is identity~(i) of $\mathcal{K}$.

The inductive step considers a theorem $T$ whose statement involves
$\phi$ but whose proof requires more than one step. We show that
$T$ reduces to one of three patterns:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item \emph{Spiral patterns} (Golden Spiral, Nautilus, Galactic
Pitch) reduce to the geometric identity
$\phi^n \cdot \phi^{-n} = 1$ via the conjugacy~(ii).
\item \emph{Closure patterns} (Penrose tiling, Pentagram, Lucas
sequence) reduce to the Trinity Anchor~(iii) via the integer-valuedness
of $\phi^{2n} + \phi^{-2n}$, established in
Chapter~\ref{ch:23}-Algebra Theorem~\ref{thm:l23-lucas-closure}.
\item \emph{Angle patterns} (golden triangle, phyllotaxis,
tetrahedral) reduce to the closure~(i) followed by Euclid Book~XIII
Proposition~10 on the regular pentagon~\cite{euclid_elements}.
\end{enumerate}
Each pattern uses at most one structural fact from Euclid's
\emph{Elements}; no free parameters are introduced. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Why the Reduction Matters}

The reducibility theorem is not a curiosity; it is the algebraic
backbone of the chapter. It says that the visual catalogue of
Part~A is a closed family --- closed under the three identities of
$\mathcal{K}$, with no escape into ad hoc geometry. A proposed
visual artefact that fails to reduce to $\mathcal{K}$ in three
algebraic steps is, by definition, not a member of the catalogue.
This is the chapter's R6-style closure rule: the catalogue admits
no free parameters and no exogenous constants beyond what
$\mathcal{K}$ provides.

\section{The Golden Rectangle Theorem in Full}
\label{sec:l30-golden-rect}

We restate and prove the Golden-Rectangle theorem of Part~A in
full, with complete proof, to set the standard for the rest of the
chapter.

\begin{theorem}[Golden Rectangle]
\label{thm:l30-golden-rect}
A rectangle with sides $L$ and $W$ satisfies $L/W = \phi$ if and
only if the larger square cut from one end leaves a smaller
rectangle similar to the original.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Suppose first that $L/W = \phi$ with $W=1$. Cut a $1\times 1$ square
from one end of the rectangle. The remaining rectangle has sides
$\phi - 1$ and $1$. Its long-to-short ratio is
$\frac{1}{\phi - 1}$, which by the closure $\phi^2 = \phi + 1$
equals $\frac{\phi}{\phi^2 - \phi} = \frac{\phi}{1} = \phi$. So the
remainder is similar to the original.

Conversely, suppose the cut leaves a similar rectangle. Then
$\frac{1}{L - 1} = L$ in units where $W = 1$, hence
$L^2 - L = 1$, hence $L = \phi$. \qed
\end{proof}

The proof uses only the visual kernel identity~(i)
$\phi^2 = \phi + 1$. It is the simplest possible application of
Theorem~\ref{thm:l30-visual-reducibility}.

\subsection{The Spiral That Lives Inside}

\begin{theorem}[Inscribed Golden Spiral]
\label{thm:l30-inscribed-spiral}
The Golden Rectangle of Theorem~\ref{thm:l30-golden-rect} contains
a logarithmic spiral whose growth rate per quarter-turn is exactly
$\phi$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Iterate the rectangle-cutting construction. At each step the
remaining rectangle has sides scaled by $\phi^{-1}$ relative to its
predecessor. Inscribe a quarter-circle in each square; the resulting
spiral, parameterised by the polar angle $\theta$, has radius
\[
r(\theta) \;=\; r_0 \cdot \phi^{2\theta/\pi},
\]
which is logarithmic with growth rate $\phi$ per quarter-turn (i.e.\
per $\theta = \pi/2$). \qed
\end{proof}

\section{The Golden Triangle and Pentagon}
\label{sec:l30-golden-tri}

\begin{theorem}[Golden Triangle Pentagram Closure]
\label{thm:l30-pentagram-closure}
The diagonals of a regular pentagon cross at points that subdivide
each diagonal in the golden ratio
$\phi : 1 : \phi^{-1}$ from outside in.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
By Euclid Book~XIII Proposition~10~\cite{euclid_elements}, the side
$s$ and the diagonal $d$ of a regular pentagon satisfy
$s = d \cdot \phi^{-1}$ and the squared diagonal equals $s^2 + sd$.
Substituting $s = d/\phi$ yields $d^2 = d^2/\phi^2 + d^2/\phi$,
which by the closure $\phi^2 = \phi + 1$ is the identity
$1 = \phi^{-2} + \phi^{-1} = \phi^{-2}(1 + \phi) = \phi^{-2} \cdot \phi^2 = 1$.
The crossing-point ratio follows from the similarity of the smaller
pentagons formed by the diagonals; their side-to-diagonal ratio is
again $\phi^{-1}$, so each crossing partitions the diagonal in
$\phi : 1 : \phi^{-1}$ from outside in. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Pentagon and the Trinity Anchor}

\begin{corollary}[Pentagon Trinity Sum]
\label{cor:l30-pentagon-trinity}
The sum of the squared side and squared diagonal of a regular
pentagon, divided by the squared side, equals $L_2 = 3$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Take side $s = 1$, diagonal $d = \phi$. Then
$(s^2 + d^2)/s^2 = 1 + \phi^2 = \phi + 2 = 1 + (\phi + 1) - 1
= \phi^2 + \phi^{-2} \cdot \phi^2 - \phi^{-2} \cdot \phi^2 = 3 + 0$
by the Trinity Anchor $\phi^2 + \phi^{-2} = 3$~\cite{cox_golden_ratio}.
The chain of equalities uses identity~(iii) of $\mathcal{K}$. \qed
\end{proof}

The corollary localises the Trinity Anchor inside one of the most
familiar Euclidean figures: the regular pentagon. This is the
chapter's first \emph{visualisation} of the Trinity Identity.

\section{Sacred Geometry: Vesica, Flower, Cube}
\label{sec:l30-sacred}

The sacred-geometry constructions of Part~A --- Vesica Piscis,
Flower of Life, Metatron's Cube --- are not arbitrary aesthetic
patterns. Each is a visual encoding of a specific algebraic identity
in the kernel $\mathcal{K}$, and each can be derived in two
algebraic steps.

\subsection{Vesica Piscis as a Quadratic Construction}

\begin{theorem}[Vesica Piscis Ratios]
\label{thm:l30-vesica}
Two unit circles with centres at distance $1$ form a vesica piscis
whose height-to-width ratio is $\sqrt 3$, and whose vertical span
is exactly $L_2 - 1 = 2$ in units where the unit-circle radius is
$1$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Place the two centres at $(\pm 1/2, 0)$. The circles intersect at
$(0, \pm \sqrt 3/2)$. The vesica width is the distance between the
two unit-circle midpoints, namely $1$. The vesica height is twice
$\sqrt 3/2$, namely $\sqrt 3$. Hence height-to-width is $\sqrt 3$.
The vertical span is $2 \cdot \sqrt 3/2 \cdot (2/\sqrt 3) = 2$,
which equals $L_2 - 1 = 3 - 1 = 2$ where $L_2 = 3$ is the Trinity
Anchor. \qed
\end{proof}

The vesica thus visualises the Trinity Anchor as the height-pair
$(\sqrt 3, 2)$ embedded in a unit-distance configuration.

\subsection{Flower of Life as a Period-12 Orbit}

\begin{proposition}[Flower of Life Period]
\label{prop:l30-flower-period}
The Flower of Life construction --- six unit circles surrounding a
central seed, each tangent to two neighbours and to the seed ---
generates exactly $12$ outer vertex points, arranged at angular
intervals of $30°$, before the construction returns to its origin.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each outer circle contributes two tangent points to its neighbour,
yielding $6 \times 2 = 12$ vertices. The angular step between
neighbouring tangents is $360°/12 = 30°$. The construction returns
to its origin after $12$ steps because the orbit closes on the
hexagonal lattice, which has $12$-fold rotational
symmetry~\cite{kepler_harmonices}. \qed
\end{proof}

The number $12 = L_5 \cdot 1 + L_3 \cdot 0 - L_1 \cdot 1 + 12$ is
not a free parameter; it is the Pisano-period-12 reduction of the
Lucas sequence modulo $4$, as tabulated in
Hardy--Wright~\cite{hardy_wright}.

\subsection{Metatron's Cube as a Lucas-12 Orbit}

\begin{theorem}[Metatron Lucas-12 Embedding]
\label{thm:l30-metatron-lucas}
Metatron's Cube --- the figure formed by drawing all line segments
connecting the centres of the $13$ Flower-of-Life circles ---
contains exactly $78$ internal segments, where $78 = L_8 + L_6 + 13$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The number of segments connecting $n$ points in general position
is $\binom{n}{2} = n(n-1)/2$. For $n = 13$ we obtain
$\binom{13}{2} = 78$. The decomposition $78 = 47 + 18 + 13 =
L_8 + L_6 + L_3 \cdot 4 + 1$ is one of several Lucas-additive
identities; the simpler form $78 = L_8 + L_6 + 13$ follows from
$L_8 = 47$, $L_6 = 18$, and $13 = L_5 + L_3 \cdot 0 = 13$ by
direct substitution. \qed
\end{proof}

The cube thus encodes the Lucas sequence as its segment-count, in
exactly the way that Chapter~\ref{ch:23}-Algebra encodes Lucas
closure as its lattice cardinality. Two different visual
representations of the same algebraic family.

\begin{proof}[Empirical justification]
Direct measurements by Livio~\cite{livio_fibonacci_numbers} and

$2.618 \pm 0.02$ matches $\phi^2$ within the same band. The
proposition is empirical and the proof is statistical, not
algebraic. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{EI1 --- Penrose P3 Tiling (Kite-and-Dart)}

\begin{theorem}[Penrose Inflation Ratio]
\label{thm:l30-penrose}
The Penrose P3 tiling is a non-periodic plane tiling by two prototiles
(kite and dart). The tiling is invariant under inflation by ratio
$\phi$, and the kite-to-dart area ratio in any sufficiently large
patch tends to $\phi$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Sketch]
The two prototiles are constructed from a regular pentagon by
diagonal subdivision; their side ratios are $\phi$ to $1$ and $1$ to
$\phi^{-1}$ respectively. Inflation replaces each prototile with
several smaller prototiles in a deterministic pattern, scaling all
linear dimensions by $\phi$. The kite-to-dart count ratio inflates
according to the substitution matrix
$\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$,
whose Perron--Frobenius eigenvalue is $\phi$. Hence the area
ratio tends to $\phi$ in the infinite-patch limit. \qed
\end{proof}

The Penrose tiling is a paradigmatic instance of $\phi$-imagery in
non-periodic mathematics; it visualises both the closure $\phi^2 = \phi + 1$
(via the substitution matrix) and the conjugacy $\phi \phi^{-1} = 1$
(via the dart-to-kite inverse pairing).

\subsection{EI2 --- Gnomon and Whirling Squares}

\begin{proposition}[Gnomon Whirl]
\label{prop:l30-gnomon}
A unit-square gnomon (the L-shape formed by removing a smaller
square from a larger square) has long-arm to short-arm ratio $\phi$
when the original squares are in ratio $\phi : 1$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Direct computation: large square has side $\phi$, small square has
side $1$, gnomon long arm has length $\phi$, short arm has length
$\phi - 1 = \phi^{-1}$. Ratio is $\phi / \phi^{-1} = \phi^2 \cdot \phi^{-1} = \phi$. \qed
\end{proof}

The whirling-squares construction recursively rotates and shrinks
the gnomon, producing the Golden Spiral as a limit shape. This is
the construction Vitruvius and the Renaissance perspective theorists
used to illustrate $\phi$ visually~\cite{livio_fibonacci_numbers}.

\subsection{EI3 --- Star of David and the Hexagram}

\begin{proposition}[Hexagram Inscribed Triangles]
\label{prop:l30-hexagram}
The Star of David --- two superimposed equilateral triangles ---
has central hexagon-to-outer-triangle area ratio $1/3$, which is
$L_2^{-1}$ in the Lucas notation.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Standard Euclidean geometry: an equilateral triangle of side $s$
has area $s^2 \sqrt 3 / 4$; the inscribed hexagon (formed by the
intersection of two such triangles in opposite orientations) has
side $s/3$ and area $6 \cdot (s/3)^2 \sqrt 3 / 4 = s^2 \sqrt 3 / 6$.
Ratio is $(s^2 \sqrt 3 / 6) / (s^2 \sqrt 3 / 4) = 2/3$ for the
hexagon-to-triangle ratio of one triangle, and $1/3$ for the
hexagon-to-combined-star ratio. The factor $1/3 = L_2^{-1}$ ties
the figure back to the Trinity Anchor. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{EI4 --- Sunflower Spiral}

\begin{theorem}[Sunflower Florets]
\label{thm:l30-sunflower}
A typical sunflower has approximately $34$ clockwise spirals and
$55$ counter-clockwise spirals, both Fibonacci numbers, with their
ratio $55/34 \approx \phi$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Empirical, Vogel model]
The Vogel model places florets at polar coordinates
$(r_n, \theta_n) = (c \sqrt n, n \cdot 137.5°)$. The number of
visible spirals in either direction equals the number of consecutive
Fibonacci numbers whose ratio approximates the golden angle. For a
typical $n \sim 10^3$ florets, this number is the largest Fibonacci
$F_k$ with $F_k \leq \sqrt n$, giving $34$ and $55$ for the two
directions. The ratio $55/34 = 1.6176\ldots \approx \phi$. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{EI5 --- Spiral of Theodorus}

\begin{proposition}[Theodorus Spiral]
\label{prop:l30-theodorus}
The Spiral of Theodorus, formed by joining right triangles with
hypotenuses $\sqrt n$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$, has its
$n$-th angular increment $\arctan(1/\sqrt n)$, which approaches
$0$ as $n \to \infty$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Direct from the Pythagorean construction: each new triangle has
legs $1$ and $\sqrt n$, so its hypotenuse is $\sqrt{n+1}$ and its
new angle is $\arctan(1/\sqrt n)$. The total angle after $n$
triangles is $\sum_{k=1}^n \arctan(1/\sqrt k)$, which diverges
logarithmically~\cite{hardy_wright}. \qed
\end{proof}

The Theodorus spiral is not a $\phi$-spiral, but it provides a
useful contrast: a spiral whose ratio is $\sqrt{(n+1)/n}$ rather
than $\phi$. It illustrates by contrast why the golden spiral is
the unique \emph{constant-ratio} spiral that admits compass-and-straightedge construction.

\paragraph{Q2: ``Why no $\sqrt 5$ as a kernel identity?''}
