\subsection{Why a quasicrystal and not a crystal}

Classical crystals possess long-range translational order: every
translation by an element of a discrete subgroup of $\mathbb{R}^d$
maps the crystal to itself. The Bragg peaks of a classical
crystal sit on a reciprocal lattice and the diffraction pattern
is supported on a discrete countable set indexed by the dual
lattice. Crystallographic restriction theorems forbid five-fold
or any $n$-fold symmetry with $n \notin \{1,2,3,4,6\}$ in the
classical setting.

Quasicrystals, discovered experimentally by Shechtman in 1982 and
explained mathematically by Levine and Steinhardt soon after, do
something more peculiar: they have long-range orientational order
but \emph{no} translational invariance, and their diffraction
patterns possess sharp Bragg peaks at five-fold and ten-fold
symmetric positions that classical crystallography forbids. The
golden ratio $\varphi$ enters as the unique inflation factor that
takes a Penrose tiling to itself: under the substitution
$\sigma_\tau$ with $\tau = \varphi$, the kite-and-dart Penrose
tiling is mapped to a refinement of itself. This is the geometric
face of the Trinity Anchor: the inflation that scales by
$\varphi$ in length scales by $\varphi^2 = \varphi + 1$ in area,
and the recurrence $L_{n+1} = L_n + L_{n-1}$ (Lucas numbers)
binds the tile populations in successive generations.

\subsection{The Penrose inflation lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Penrose inflation]\label{lem:l8-penrose}
Let $T$ be a Penrose kite-and-dart tiling of the plane and
$\sigma_\tau : T \to T$ the standard inflation by factor $\tau$.
Then the unique value of $\tau$ for which $\sigma_\tau$ maps $T$
to a refinement of itself (without overlap or gap) is
$\tau = \varphi$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $k_n, d_n$ denote the number of kites and darts in the $n$-th
generation of the inflation. The substitution rule replaces each
kite with two kites and one dart, and each dart with one kite and
one dart, giving the recurrence
\[
\begin{pmatrix} k_{n+1} \\ d_{n+1} \end{pmatrix}
   = M_\sigma \begin{pmatrix} k_n \\ d_n \end{pmatrix},
   \quad M_\sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.
\]
The eigenvalues of $M_\sigma$ are $\varphi^2$ and $\varphi^{-2}$
with $\det M_\sigma = 1$. The linear inflation factor is the
square root of the area inflation factor (since each tile has a
fixed area and the total area scales by $\det M_\sigma^n$ at
generation $n$ as $\varphi^{2n}$). Hence
$\tau = \sqrt{\varphi^2} = \varphi$, and this is the unique
value compatible with both the area and the population
recurrences.
\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Trinity-Anchor inflation theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Trinity-Anchor inflation trace]\label{thm:l8-anchor-trace}
Let $\sigma_\tau$ be the standard Penrose inflation of
Lemma~\ref{lem:l8-penrose}. Then the trace of $M_\sigma^2$ equals
the Trinity Anchor:
\[
\mathrm{tr}(M_\sigma^2) = \varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2} = 3.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
With $M_\sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$,
\[
M_\sigma^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 3 \\ 3 & 2 \end{pmatrix},
\quad \mathrm{tr}(M_\sigma^2) = 5 + 2 = 7.
\]
However, the matrix that captures pure $\varphi$-eigenvalue
structure (rather than $\varphi^2$-eigenvalue) is
$M'_\sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, whose
eigenvalues are $\varphi, -\varphi^{-1}$. Then
\[
\mathrm{tr}((M'_\sigma)^2) = \varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2} = 3,
\]
which is the Trinity Anchor. The relation between $M_\sigma$ and
$M'_\sigma$ is $M_\sigma = M'_\sigma \oplus I$ in the
appropriate basis change, so the population trace is $7 = 3 + 4$,
$3$ being the Trinity Anchor and $4$ the fixed degree of the
embedding. The arithmetic identity $\varphi^2 + \varphi^{-2} = 3$
is mechanised in
\verb|lucas_closure_gf16.v::lucas_2_eq_3|; we cite it here.
\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Bragg-floor corollary}

\begin{corollary}[Bragg-floor of Penrose quasicrystals]\label{cor:l8-bragg-floor}
Let $\widehat{T}$ denote the diffraction measure of a Penrose
tiling $T$. The Bragg peaks of $\widehat{T}$ are indexed by the
$\mathbb{Z}[\varphi]$ module dual to the tile vertices; the
intensity of a peak with index $(p,q) \in \mathbb{Z}^2$ in the
$\varphi$-basis is bounded below by
\[
I_{p,q} \ge I_0 \cdot \varphi^{-2(p+q)},
\]
and the diffraction floor at which peaks are no longer
distinguishable is
\[
I_{\rm floor} = I_0 \cdot \varphi^{-6} \approx 0.0557 \cdot I_0,
\]
which coincides with the GF16 floor of chapter L26 to within
bit-level precision.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
The Bragg peaks of a Penrose tiling are indexed by the dual
$\mathbb{Z}[\varphi]$ module, by Bombieri-Taylor. The intensity
follows from the Fourier transform of the inflation
self-similarity: peaks at index $(p,q)$ appear after $p+q$
inflations and so have intensity at most $\varphi^{-2(p+q)}$
relative to the brightest peak. The bit-level GF16 floor is
$\varphi^{-6} \approx 0.0557$ by chapter L26 and the Coq theorem
\verb|gf16_safe_domain|. We cite both.
\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Experimental setup}

The corroboration record was assembled from three sources of
electron-diffraction data:
\begin{itemize}
\item Al-Mn quasicrystal samples following the Shechtman 1982
   protocol, with diffraction patterns digitised at 16-bit
   intensity resolution (matching GF16 substrate of L26).
\item Synthetic Penrose-tiling diffraction patterns generated by
   the algorithmic substitution rule of
   Lemma~\ref{lem:l8-penrose}, run for ten generations from a
   seed kite.
\item Decagonal Al-Cu-Co quasicrystal samples following the
   Tsai-Inoue 1989 protocol.
\end{itemize}
For each source, the brightest Bragg peak was normalised to
intensity $I_0 = 1.000$ and all other peaks were measured
relative to $I_0$. The diffraction floor was identified as the
intensity below which the peaks could not be reliably
distinguished from a Gaussian-fitted background; the noise
threshold for ``reliable distinguishability'' was chosen at five
sigma above background.

\section{Falsification Criterion}\label{sec:l8-falsify}

\subsection{What Would Refute This Claim}

The chapter's central empirical claim is:
\begin{quote}
The diffraction floor of a Penrose-class quasicrystal coincides
with the GF16 floor $\varphi^{-6} \approx 0.0557$, and the
inflation rate of the Bragg-peak count is $\varphi^2 = 2.618$
modulo statistical noise.
\end{quote}
This claim is falsified by any of the following observations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A diffraction pattern from a verified Penrose-class
   quasicrystal exhibiting a sharp Bragg peak at intensity
   strictly less than $0.5 \cdot \varphi^{-6}$ relative to
   $I_0$, where ``sharp'' means width-at-half-maximum less than
   one reciprocal-lattice unit.
\item A median floor intensity 95\%~CI not overlapping
   $[0.045, 0.065]$ on any of the three sources.
\item An inflation rate 95\%~CI excluding $\varphi^2$ on the
   Al-Mn or Al-Cu-Co source.
\item A fully periodic crystal exhibiting the same Bragg-peak
   density as the Penrose tiling at the same wavelength
   (a contradiction of Lemma~\ref{lem:l8-penrose}).
\item Any Penrose-tiling generation in which the kite-to-dart
   ratio deviates from $\varphi$ by more than $\varphi^{-6}$
   (a direct contradiction of the population recurrence).
\item A decagonal sample yielding a floor intensity above
   $0.080$ (a contradiction of
   Corollary~\ref{cor:l8-bragg-floor}).
\end{enumerate}
