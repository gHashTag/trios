\section{The Single Source}

We open the monograph with a single irrational number.
\[
  \varphi \;=\; \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \;\approx\; 1.618{,}033{,}988{,}749{,}894{,}8.
\]
The whole architecture of the dissertation is the unfolding of one
algebraic identity over \(\varphi\):

\begin{theorem}[Trinity Identity]\label{thm:trinity-identity-prologue}
  Let \(\varphi\) be the positive root of \(x^{2} - x - 1 = 0\). Then
  \[
    \varphi^{2} + \varphi^{-2} \;=\; 3.
  \]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  From \(\varphi^{2} = \varphi + 1\) and \(\varphi^{-2} = 2 - \varphi\)
  (both immediate from \(\varphi^{2} - \varphi - 1 = 0\)),
  \(\varphi^{2} + \varphi^{-2} = (\varphi + 1) + (2 - \varphi) = 3\).
  \qed
\end{proof}

This is the gate of the monograph. Every later chapter pulls one
strand from this identity and follows it to its empirical, geometric,
or computational consequence.

\section{L0 Strand II --- Formalisation: The Trinity Identity}
\label{sec:l0-strand-II}

Strand II makes the Trinity Identity formally rigorous. We state
and prove the identity, and we cite the Q1/Q2 references that
support its derivation.

\begin{theorem}[Trinity Identity]
\label{thm:l0-trinity-identity}
The golden ratio $\varphi = (1 + \sqrt{5})/2$ satisfies
\[
  \varphi^{2} + \varphi^{-2} = 3.
\]
Equivalently, $\varphi^{2} + \varphi^{-2} = L_{2}$, where $L_{2}
= 3$ is the second Lucas number.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We compute directly. Since $\varphi$ satisfies $\varphi^{2} =
\varphi + 1$, we have $\varphi^{2} = \varphi + 1$. Multiplying
$\varphi^{2} - \varphi - 1 = 0$ by $\varphi^{-2}$ gives $1 -
\varphi^{-1} - \varphi^{-2} = 0$, so $\varphi^{-2} = 1 - \varphi^{-1}$.

Now $\varphi - 1 = \varphi^{-1}$ (the defining property of the
golden ratio: $\varphi$ and $\varphi^{-1}$ differ by $1$), so
$\varphi^{-1} = \varphi - 1$ and therefore $\varphi^{-2} = 1 -
(\varphi - 1) = 2 - \varphi$. Adding:
\[
  \varphi^{2} + \varphi^{-2} = (\varphi + 1) + (2 - \varphi) = 3.
\]
This proves the identity. The same identity is proven formally in
Coq at \texttt{lucas\_closure\_gf16.v} line 87 as theorem
\texttt{lucas\_2\_eq\_3}, with status Proven.
\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Lucas Recurrence and the Anchor]
\label{thm:l0-lucas-recurrence}
The Lucas numbers $L_{n}$, defined by $L_{0} = 2, L_{1} = 1, L_{n+2}
= L_{n+1} + L_{n}$, satisfy $L_{n} = \varphi^{n} + (-\varphi)^{-n}$.
In particular, $L_{2} = \varphi^{2} + \varphi^{-2} = 3$, anchoring
Theorem~\ref{thm:l0-trinity-identity}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The closed form $L_{n} = \varphi^{n} + \psi^{n}$ where
$\psi = -\varphi^{-1} = (1 - \sqrt{5})/2$ follows from the standard
Binet-like derivation \cite{binet_formula} for the Lucas numbers.
We verify: $L_{0} = \varphi^{0} + \psi^{0} = 1 + 1 = 2$ ✓; $L_{1}
= \varphi + \psi = \varphi - \varphi^{-1} = 1$ ✓; and for $n \geq
2$, the recurrence $L_{n+2} = L_{n+1} + L_{n}$ holds because
$\varphi$ and $\psi$ both satisfy $x^{2} = x + 1$.

For $n = 2$: $L_{2} = \varphi^{2} + \psi^{2} = \varphi^{2} +
\varphi^{-2} = 3$ by Theorem~\ref{thm:l0-trinity-identity}. This
establishes that the algebraic and recurrence definitions agree.
\qed
\end{proof}

\section{Appendix L0.A --- Geometric Origins of $\varphi$}
\label{sec:l0-appendix-A}

The Pythagoreans of the 5th century BCE encountered $\varphi$ in
their study of the regular pentagon. The diagonal-to-side ratio of
the regular pentagon is $\varphi$. We sketch the proof.

Let the regular pentagon have side $1$ and diagonal $d$. Two
diagonals from adjacent vertices intersect at a point $P$ that
divides each diagonal in the ratio $1:\varphi^{-1}$. By similar
triangles formed at $P$, we have $d / 1 = 1 / (d - 1)$, which
yields $d^{2} - d - 1 = 0$, hence $d = \varphi$.

This geometric fact is recorded in Euclid's \emph{Elements} Book VI
\cite{euclid_elements}. The identification of $d / 1 = \varphi$ as
``the extreme and mean ratio'' is one of Euclid's central
constructions, and it provides the geometric anchor for the
algebraic identity $\varphi^{2} = \varphi + 1$.

= 4 (-1)^{n}$.

For $n = 2$: $L_{2}^{2} - 5 F_{2}^{2} = 9 - 5 = 4 = 4 \cdot 1$,
which checks out.
